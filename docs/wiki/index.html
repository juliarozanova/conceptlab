The one-paragraph version

We want to know whether interpretability methods — tools that claim to explain what a neural network is doing — actually tell the truth. On real data there is no answer key: if a method says a fraud model flagged a payment "because of the burst of transactions", nobody can check. So we build small synthetic worlds where the answer key is known by construction: we decide exactly which concepts exist, how they combine into labels, and how strongly each one matters. Then we train models on that world, run the interpretability methods, and grade them. A method that fails where we know the answer should not be trusted where we don't.

What's a "concept" here? A human-meaningful, true-or-false property of a transaction in its context: card_not_present, short_burst ("several transactions in a few minutes"), on_holiday ("this customer is currently traveling"). Concepts are the vocabulary in which we want explanations — a fraud analyst thinks in concepts, not in neuron activations.

The pipeline at a glance

Why synthetic data at all?

Three properties you cannot get from real payment data, no matter how much of it you have:

  • An answer key. Real fraud labels tell you that a payment was fraud, never why. Here, every fraudulent event carries the concepts that define its typology — so "explain this decision" has a checkable correct answer.
  • Counterfactuals. We can re-run any customer's history with one thing changed — the holiday removed, the burst suppressed — and watch what the model does. That is the gold-standard question ("what would have happened without X?") answered exactly, which real data can never do. (How this works →)
  • Controlled correlations. In real data, "burst of transactions" and "fraud" are tangled together. Here we deliberately plant innocent bursts (shopping sprees) and innocent travel so that each concept exists independently of fraud at a strength we choose. This is what stops concept detectors from being fraud detectors in disguise.
And the honest limitation. Synthetic worlds are simpler than reality. Results here transfer as failure modes and method rankings ("linear methods miss interactions", "TCAV leaks onto confounders"), not as absolute numbers. A method that passes here can still fail on production data; a method that fails here should not be trusted anywhere. Think of it as a driving test: passing doesn't make you a Formula 1 driver, but failing means you don't get the keys.
