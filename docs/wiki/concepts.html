The concept catalog

Every concept the synthetic world can label — 86 concepts across five families. Cards marked implemented exist in fraudgen v1; the rest are specified in the domain-concepts plan.
Overview How the data is generated Concept catalog Making interpretability deductions
How to read a card. Each box gives the plain-English definition, why fraud analysts care, and — where it changes what we can conclude — what the concept lets us test. The thin red bar shows the target correlation band: how strongly this concept is allowed to co-occur with fraud in the generated data. The band matters because the generator also plants a legitimate pathway for every concept (shown in the footer): bursts also come from shopping trips, new devices also come from phone upgrades. Without those innocent twins, a detector for any concept would secretly just be a fraud detector — and every "concept explanation" would be circular.
Stats note — "correlation" here. We use correlation loosely to mean "how much more often this concept is true on fraud than on legitimate transactions", scaled to 0–1. A band of 0.05–0.15 means "barely informative"; 0.7–0.9 means "almost always fraud when present". The point of bands (rather than forcing everything to zero) is realism: some concepts, like impossible_travel, really are near-proof of fraud, and a benchmark that pretends otherwise teaches methods the wrong lesson.

Transaction-intrinsic (level 0) — 32 concepts

Row-level facts readable off a single transaction — the payment channel, the amount's shape, the merchant's category, the geography, and (new in this taxonomy) the verification signals: CVV, address checks, 3-D Secure, PIN, decline reason codes. These are the "identity concepts": each is simultaneously an input column and a concept, which is what lets the testbed compare input-level and concept-level attribution on the same footing.

Interpretability deduction: level-0 concepts are the easy case — any method that can't find and rank these has no business with the harder ones. They also include deliberately protective (negative-attribution) and null concepts, testing that methods can say "this made fraud less likely" and "this didn't matter at all".

Behavioral / windowed (level 1) — 17 concepts

Patterns over the recent sequence — bursts, retries, escalation, novelty, and physical impossibilities. None of these exist in a single row; they are properties of order and timing.

Interpretability deduction: these concepts separate genuinely sequential models from bag-of-features ones. If a method attributes a decision to micro_then_large, it is implicitly claiming the model used order — and because we planted the pattern, we can check that claim exactly.

short_burstL1 · windowimplemented
Several transactions squeezed into a short window.
Bursts are how card-testing looks — and how a Saturday shopping trip looks. The generator plants both, so a burst CAV encodes burstiness, not fraud.
corr band 0.10–0.25 · legit pathway: shopping sessions, checkout retries
rapid_fireL1 · window
Three or more transactions less than a minute apart.
Human hands don't move that fast; scripts do. Much stronger than short_burst — the pair tests whether methods track signal strength, not just presence.
corr band 0.30–0.50 · legit pathway: double-tap retries (rare)
multi_merchant_burstL1 · window

Testers rotate merchants to dodge per-merchant velocity rules; mall crawls do the same innocently.
corr band 0.25–0.40 · legit pathway: market/mall trips (planted)
same_merchant_retryL1 · window
Repeated attempts at one merchant within minutes.
Usually a failed payment being retried; occasionally a fraudster hammering one door.
corr band 0.15–0.30 · legit pathway: payment retries
escalating_amountsL1 · window

Fraudsters probe upward to find the card's ceiling.

Entity-history & latent-state (level 2) — 19 concepts

Deviations from an entity's own long-run profile (customer, merchant, or device), plus concepts that are really latent world-states the generator knows about (travel episodes, life events, merchant breaches). Latents deserve special attention: on_holiday is not computable from any row — it is a hidden cause. The generator exports it as truth and can re-simulate the world with it switched off (a counterfactual), which is how we measure its real causal effect.

Interpretability deduction: this family carries the confounding tests. Travel causes foreign, unusual-looking activity without causing fraud; a faithful method must attribute fraud decisions to the fraud-causing concepts and not the innocent latent riding alongside them.

Not 'is €800 a lot?' but 'is €800 a lot *for Maria*?' Tests whether models build per-entity baselines — and whether attribution credits the deviation, not the raw amount.

Fraud-pattern composites (level 3a) — 7 concepts

Named combinations of lower concepts — the "chords" analysts actually recognize: test-then-cash-out, the takeover signature, cash-conversion channels. Written in the same logic DSL, so their internal structure (AND vs OR) is known exactly.

Interpretability deduction: compounds make credit assignment testable at depth. ANDs create interaction effects (each part is worthless alone — linear attribution fails); ORs create overdetermination (single-toggle importance collapses to zero and fair-share definitions like Shapley become necessary). Both failure modes are planted on purpose.

test_then_cashoutL3 · pattern
The full card-testing arc: tiny probes (often failing CVV), then a big spend.
A compound concept whose parts are themselves concepts. The benchmark's central question: do methods credit the compound, its parts, or double-count both?
composition: micro_then_large ∧ card_not_present ∧ (cvv_failure ∨ declined in window)
enumeration_signatureL3 · pattern
One merchant being hammered by many cards in rapid, CVV-failing succession.
Lives on the merchant side — invisible in any single customer's history. Separates merchant-aware architectures from customer-sequence ones.
composition: many-cards-one-merchant latent ∧ rapid_fire ∧ cvv_failure
geo_takeover_signatureL3 · pattern
New device + unfamiliar country + failing address/3DS checks.
The ATO chord: each note is weak, the chord is loud. Tests interaction-aware attribution (linear methods hear only notes).
composition: new_device ∧ unusual_country ∧ (avs_mismatch ∨ three_ds_failed)
bustout_rampL3 · pattern
A young account ramping spend and utilization toward a cliff.
Long-horizon compound: none of its parts fire on any single event.
composition: spend_trend_ramp ∧ credit_util_spike ∧ account_young
cash_conversionL3 · pattern
Any of the 'turn credit into cash' channels.
A disjunction (OR) — the overdetermination generator. When two channels fire at once, single-toggle importance goes to zero and Shapley-style credit is required.
composition: fenceable_mcc ∨ gift_card_mcc ∨ crypto_mcc ∨ atm_withdrawal
verification_evasionL3 · pattern
Any route around strong verification.
Another disjunction, verification edition.
composition: magstripe_fallback ∨ three_ds_failed ∨ moto
laundering_patternL3 · pattern
Round amounts moving fast through transfer rails.
A conjunction of individually-benign concepts — the anti-pattern of geo_takeover: here no part should get much solo credit.
composition: round_amount ∧ money_transfer_mcc ∧ velocity_spike

Typologies (level 3b) — 11 concepts

The eleven fraud storylines the generator can inject, each hijacking a customer's (or merchant's) stream. Every typology declares its defining concept set — the ground-truth answer to "why is this transaction fraud?" — which is what fraud-model explanations are graded against.

Interpretability deduction: because different typologies use different concepts, no single global concept ranking can be right — explanations must be local. The typology layer is what turns that from an opinion into a measurable property of the benchmark.

card_testingL3 · patternimplemented
A batch of stolen card numbers being validated with rapid micro-charges.
The highest-volume fraud pattern in the wild.
defining concepts: rapid_fire, micro_amount, card_not_present, cvv_failure
enumeration_attackL3 · pattern
A BIN attack: guessing card numbers in sequence against one merchant.
Pure merchant-side signal — the planted reason merchant embeddings should win benchmarks.
defining concepts: enumeration_signature, micro_amount
stolen_card_cnpL3 · pattern

The cash-out that follows testing; the bread-and-butter CNP fraud story.
defining concepts: test_then_cashout, cash_conversion, unusual_country
counterfeit_posL3 · pattern
Cloned physical cards used in person after a skimming breach.
The typology that requires CPP exposure — relational evidence across customers.
defining concepts: cpp_exposed, magstripe_fallback, impossible_travel
lost_stolen_posL3 · pattern
A physically lost/stolen card used before the owner notices.
Old-fashioned and fast: cash and goods before the freeze.

account_takeoverL3 · patternimplemented
Credentials stolen; the attacker becomes the customer.
The long-baseline typology: everything looks plausible except against months of history.

synthetic_identity_bustoutL3 · pattern
A fabricated identity nurtures good behavior, then maxes out and vanishes.

merchant_bustoutL3 · pattern
A merchant builds plausible volume, spikes with stolen-card traffic, and dies.

transaction_launderingL3 · pattern
A tail merchant washing payments for an unseen business.
The quiet typology: individually boring events, collectively a pipe.

refund_abuseL3 · pattern
Systematic exploitation of refund flows, mostly in digital goods.
First-party-adjacent: the 'customer' is real, the claims aren't.
defining concepts: same_merchant_retry (refund rail), digital_goods
first_party_friendlyL3 · patternimplemented
A real customer disputing purchases they genuinely made.
Behaviorally identical to innocence — zero defining concepts. The Bayes floor that keeps every method honest about its confidence.
