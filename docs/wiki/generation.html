How the data is generated

From an empty world to labeled transactions, one layer at a time. No statistics background assumed — every distribution gets a plain-language box.
Overview How the data is generated Concept catalog Making interpretability deductions

Layer 1 — a world of entities

Before any transaction exists, the generator creates the cast: customers, merchants, cards, and devices. Each customer is drawn from a behavioral archetype — "urban commuter", "online-heavy shopper", "light cash user" — which sets their typical spending amounts, favorite merchant categories, how often they transact, and their home country. Archetypes are then individually jittered so no two customers are identical: think of an archetype as a recipe and each customer as one cook's version of it.

Stats box — the lognormal distribution. Purchase amounts follow a lognormal: most purchases are small, a few are large, and none are negative. The easiest way to picture it: take ordinary bell-curve randomness, then exponentiate it. Coffee-sized amounts are common, laptop-sized amounts are rare, yacht-sized amounts are very rare — but all possible. Each merchant category has its own version (fuel amounts cluster tightly; electronics spread wide).

Merchants get a category (MCC), a country, an age, and — importantly — a popularity. Merchant popularity follows a Zipf pattern, and the taxonomy's merchant-side concepts (fresh merchants, tail merchants, volume spikes) depend on it.

Stats box — Zipf's law. A few merchants are enormous (everyone shops there), and there is a very long tail of tiny ones. Concretely: the 2nd most popular merchant gets about half the traffic of the 1st, the 10th about a tenth. Real payment data looks exactly like this, and it matters for modelling: a model must learn good representations for merchants it has seen thousands of times and merchants it has seen twice.

Layer 2 — events on a timeline

Each customer emits transactions over a simulated timeline. When they transact is as informative as what they buy, so timing is modeled carefully: people shop more at midday and on weekends, more right after payday, and — crucially — in sessions: one purchase makes another purchase in the next few minutes more likely (you're at the shops already).

Stats box — point processes and "bursty" time. A point process is just a recipe for sprinkling events along a timeline. The simplest (a Poisson process) sprinkles them independently at some rate. Ours varies that rate by hour, weekday and payday (a non-homogeneous process) and adds self-excitement (a Hawkes process): each event temporarily raises the rate, producing natural clusters. This is why the gaps between transactions have a heavy-tailed, clumpy look — and why "burst" concepts need planted innocent explanations, because the legitimate world bursts all by itself.

Latent episodes — the world's hidden switches

On top of the baseline rhythm, each customer carries a set of latent episode variables — hidden on/off switches the generator flips over time: on_trip (travel: purchases shift to a foreign country), life_event (a move or new job shifts the whole spending profile), seasonal_peak (December happens to everyone). Merchants have latents too: a breach window (cards visiting during it become cpp_exposed) and a fraud-propensity level.

Why latents matter so much for interpretability. A latent like on_trip is a hidden common cause: it makes foreign_country, new_country and velocity_spike all fire at once — with zero fraud. Any explanation method that sees those surface features co-occurring with fraud (in other customers) and attributes a traveler's flagged payment to "being abroad" is confusing correlation with causation. The generator knows the truth, so we can catch it doing that.

Layer 3 — the authorization micro-model

Real payment systems answer back: approved or declined, address check passed or failed, 3-D Secure challenged or waived. These verification signals are among the strongest features real fraud models use, so the generator simulates a small issuer rule stack: balances can be insufficient (innocent declines), stolen-card attempts fail CVV checks more often, takeovers trip address verification, and step-up rules create the thresholds that just_below_limit structuring dances under. Each outcome is written into the event row — and each becomes a level-0 concept in the catalog.

Layer 4 — fraud campaigns

Fraud is injected as campaigns: an operator with a typology (a storyline — see the catalog's level-3b family) hijacks a customer, card, or merchant and rewrites part of their stream. Card testing splices a rapid run of micro-charges; an account takeover adds a new device, a foreign IP and an escalating drain; a merchant bust-out inflates a young merchant's volume before it vanishes. Crucially the campaign interleaves with the victim's ongoing legitimate activity — fraud rides on top of real life, exactly as in production data.

Every campaign records its defining concept set — the concepts that constitute its story. This is the per-event answer key: on a card-testing event, the correct explanation cites rapid_fire, micro_amount, card_not_present, cvv_failure — and does not cite on_holiday.

Layer 5 — labels, delays, and honest noise

In reality nobody stamps "FRAUD" on a transaction at authorization time. Fraud is discovered when the cardholder disputes it — weeks later — and some fraud is never reported at all. The generator reproduces this: a reporting delay (typically ~25 days, sometimes months), a slice of fraud that never gets a label, and both label_reported (what a production model would train on) and label_true (the answer key) in the exports. Models that ignore this — training on labels that wouldn't have existed yet — get quietly optimistic results; the testbed makes that mistake visible.

Replayable randomness and counterfactuals

The generator's most important engineering property: every random draw is addressable. Each amount, each merchant choice, each timing gap is produced by a random generator keyed by (seed, customer, column, event). Ask for the same key, get the same draw — which means we can regenerate any customer's history with one thing changed and everything else identical.

Stats box — what "counterfactual" buys us. Comparing travelers to non-travelers (correlation) confuses the trip's effect with everything else that differs between those people. Re-running the same person's history with only the trip removed isolates the trip's effect exactly. Real data can never do this — you can't re-live a customer's month. A generator can, and that single capability is why the answer key is causal rather than correlational.

Scale, briefly

For the explainability suite, thousands of sequences suffice. For comparing serious sequence models (causal TabBERT variants at ~20M parameters), the domain-concepts plan targets ~50M events over 200K customers with variable histories of 64–512 events and a 100K–1M-merchant universe — big enough that architectural choices (how history is compressed, how merchant identity is embedded) genuinely separate. Same world, two sampling modes: balanced windows for explanation grading, the raw firehose for pretraining.
