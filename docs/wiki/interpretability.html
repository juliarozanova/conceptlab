The causal picture

Everything in the testbed hangs off one structural rule: the label depends on the inputs only through the concepts. Raw columns feed concepts; concepts (possibly through compound concepts) feed the label. Even "direct" effects are wrapped as trivial level-0 concepts (card_not_present is the channel column). This is called pure mediation, and it means input-level and concept-level explanation are two zoom levels of the same picture, never two competing pictures.

The answer key has three tiers

"How important was concept c for this decision?" turns out to need three increasingly precise answers, and it saves a lot of confusion to keep them separate:

TierQuestionHow it's computedHow methods are judged
1 · StructuralCould c matter at all? Is c in the label's formula (transitively)? Gate: attribution on out-of-formula concepts must be ~0. No excuses.
2 · PivotalDid c matter for this transaction? Flip c, recompute: does the label change? Gate: concepts that are locally inert (in the formula, but this example's outcome doesn't hinge on them) must get ~0 here.
3 · GradedHow much, relative to the others? Two definitions: toggle-magnitude and Shapley (below). Score: rank correlation with both definitions.
Stats box — Spearman rank correlation (the ρ in the reports). Forget the exact numbers a method outputs; do its rankings agree with the answer key's? ρ = 1 means same order, 0 means unrelated, −1 means backwards. We use rankings because different methods output numbers on incomparable scales — order is the fair common ground.

Why tier 3 needs two definitions — the OR problem

Suppose the label is fraud = (burst AND cnp) OR unusual_country and a transaction has all three true. Toggle any single concept off — the label stays 1, because the other branch still holds. So "toggle it and watch" gives every concept zero importance, on the very example they jointly caused. This is overdetermination: multiple sufficient causes, no single point of failure.

Stats box — Shapley values, restaurant-bill edition. Three friends share a meal; how do you split the bill fairly when some dishes were shared? Shapley's answer: imagine every possible order in which people could have "arrived", charge each person what they added when they arrived, and average over all orders. Applied to concepts: build the label up from nothing, adding concepts in every order, and credit each concept with what it changed when it arrived. In the OR example above, credit gets shared instead of vanishing. The testbed exports both toggle-importance and Shapley, and reports where they disagree — because where they disagree, "which concept mattered most" genuinely has no unique answer, and a benchmark that pretends otherwise is just picking a favorite definition.

What the methods under test actually do

All the concept-attribution methods share one ingredient: a CAV.

Stats box — CAVs (Concept Activation Vectors). Take the model's internal activity (its activation vector) on many examples with the concept and many without. Fit the simplest possible classifier between the two groups. The direction that classifier looks along is the CAV — "which way is more burst-like inside this model's head". Everything downstream inherits the CAV's quality, which is why the example sets must be decorrelated (see the catalog intro): if all your burst examples are frauds, your "burst" direction is actually a fraud direction, and every explanation built on it is quietly circular.
MethodIn one sentenceCharacteristic failure the testbed exposes
ICS (integrated conceptual sensitivity) Slide the concept's component from "absent" to "present", accumulating the model's sensitivity along the way (Integrated Gradients along the CAV). Inherits CAV errors; path/baseline choices are researcher degrees of freedom.
TCAV Count how often nudging along the CAV pushes the output up — a global vote. Consistently the weakest here: leaks attribution onto correlated-but-noncausal concepts (seen directly in tab_correlated) and saturates on confident models.
CAV-ablation Delete the concept's component from the activation; measure how much the output moves. Deleting a direction can drag the activation off-distribution; entangled concepts get disturbed together.
Probe-patch Set the concept's internal value to its opposite (a mini counterfactual inside the model). Only as good as the probe; strong patches can be off-manifold.
Input-aggregated Attribute to raw inputs, then roll credit up to concepts using the known formula graph. Cannot separate two concepts built from the same column — which the taxonomy plants deliberately (foreign_country vs currency_foreign).

The audit: "is the concept even in there?"

Before blaming a method for missing a concept, ask whether the model represents that concept at all. The concept audit fits a simple probe for every concept at every layer of the trained model and reports how decodable it is. This cleanly separates three situations that would otherwise be indistinguishable: the method failed; the concept isn't represented (so ~0 attribution is correct); the concept is represented only at some layers/positions (so the method must look in the right place). Rule of thumb for reading reports: attribution claims are only as strong as the audit row above them.

Which deductions are licensed?

If you observe…You may conclude…You may NOT conclude…
A method passes gates + high ρ on both tier-3 definitions, across configs Its attribution mechanism is sound in the regimes tested; failures elsewhere are likely CAV/representation issues, not mechanism. That its numbers on a production model are correct.
A method attributes to a planted confounder (e.g. on_holiday) It confuses correlation with causation — disqualifying for fraud explanations, where confounders (travel!) are everywhere. —
Toggle-GT and Shapley-GT disagree strongly on a config The label is overdetermined there; ranking has no unique answer, so compare methods on gates only. That one method is "better" because it matches your preferred definition.
Per-typology attribution works but the global ranking is ~0 Fraud is heterogeneous: explanations must be local (per case). A global "top concepts" bar chart over all fraud is a category error. That the methods failed.
A concept has low audit AUC and ~0 attribution The model likely doesn't use it — a possible model shortcoming worth investigating. That the attribution method missed it.
The transfer rule, one last time. What travels from synthetic worlds to real ones is the structure of results: which failure modes exist, which methods are immune, what must be controlled (decorrelation, audits, locality). Absolute scores do not travel. Treat every result here as a necessary condition on a method — a driving test, not a race result.
